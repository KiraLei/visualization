        Title: Misleading 3D Pie/Donut Chart Example Link: Plecto “6 Examples of Bad Data Visualizations” 

        This chart is a classic case of misleading design due to multiple avoidable flaws:
        1. 3D pie/donut distortion: The 3D perspective exaggerates slice sizes depending on depth and angle, making visual comparison unreliable—a violation of Tufte’s guidance to avoid chartjunk.
        2. Poor color choice: Adjacent slices share similar hues, making it hard to distinguish categories—worsened by weak legend labeling.
        3. Lack of context or annotation: There’s no title or data source, so users cannot assess what’s being shown or its origin—a serious clarity issue
        4. Ineffective data representation: Pie charts inherently offer low data density and poor accuracy, especially when many categories are present .
        This violates core visualization rules: use the right chart type, provide contextual clarity, and avoid misleading visual encoding . The result is a chart that confuses more than it informs.

      ```
    - How could this data visualization have been improved?  
      ```
      Your answer...

        * Switch to a bar chart: A simple 2D bar chart aligns category names on the vertical axis and uses horizontal bars to depict values, making comparisons accurate and intuitive.
        * Use accessible colors: Employ 5–8 distinct, color-blind–friendly colors, and ensure each is tied to clear labels or annotations.
        * Add contextual information: Include a meaningful title, source citation, data period, and unit of measure. Add annotations to highlight key patterns or takeaways.
        * Remove 3D styling: Flatten the design to minimize perspective distortion, ensure bars align with zero baseline, and avoid gimmicky effects.
        * Enhance clarity with labels: Directly label each bar with its numeric value, reducing reliance on the legend and improving readability.
